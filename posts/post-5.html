Performance Marketing

CAC Is Vanity. Payback Period Is Sanity. Here's Why.

April 2025
6 min read
By The Guerilla Marketer

Every founder knows their CAC. It's the metric everyone reports in board decks, investor updates, and agency performance reviews. But CAC alone is one of the most misleading numbers in your entire P&L — and optimising for it in isolation has caused more D2C businesses to quietly bleed cash than almost any other mistake.

The number you actually need to be tracking is your Payback Period. Here's why the distinction matters — and how to calculate it for your brand.

What CAC Doesn't Tell You

CAC tells you how much you spent to acquire a customer. It says nothing about how long it takes to get that money back. A ₹800 CAC with a ₹1,200 average order value and 60% gross margin sounds fine — you recoup in one order. But what if your customer only orders once? What if returns wipe 20% of that margin? What if your contribution margin after shipping and ad spend is actually ₹200?

In that scenario, you're not paying back ₹800 in one order. You're paying it back over four orders — and if order two never comes, you've lost money on that customer permanently.

💡 CAC is a cost. Payback Period is a timeline. A business that knows only its cost without knowing its timeline cannot make good scaling decisions.

The Payback Period Formula

Payback Period = CAC ÷ Monthly Contribution Margin per Customer
Result = Number of months to recover acquisition cost

Monthly Contribution Margin per Customer = (Average Revenue per customer per month) × (Gross Margin %) − (Variable costs: shipping, returns, payment gateway, retention marketing cost)

For a brand with:

  • CAC of ₹900
  • Monthly AOV of ₹1,500
  • Gross margin of 55%
  • Variable costs of ₹180/order

Monthly contribution margin = (₹1,500 × 55%) − ₹180 = ₹825 − ₹180 = ₹645 per order

Payback Period = ₹900 ÷ ₹645 = 1.4 months — excellent.

Now change one variable: CAC goes to ₹1,800 (common in competitive categories on Meta). Payback Period = ₹1,800 ÷ ₹645 = 2.8 months. Acceptable, but only if you're confident that customer buys again within 3 months. Most brands aren't.

These benchmarks assume you have the working capital to fund the gap. If your payback period is 4 months but you're running tight on cash, every new customer acquisition is a cash flow risk. This is why well-funded brands can afford longer payback periods — and bootstrapped brands cannot.

How to Shorten Your Payback Period

You have three levers — and most brands underuse at least two of them:

1. Reduce CAC

Better creative, better targeting, brand investment that lowers CPMs over time. Also: improving your conversion rate on your website/Shopify — a 0.5% improvement in CVR can drop your effective CAC by 20–30% without touching ad spend.

2. Increase Gross Margin

Negotiate supplier pricing at scale. Reduce return rates (usually a product or description problem, not a logistics problem). Reduce discounting — every 5% off your AOV is a 5% hit to margin that your CAC doesn't account for.

3. Accelerate the Second Purchase

This is the biggest lever most brands neglect. A customer who buys twice in 45 days shortens your payback period dramatically — even if their second order is smaller. Build a post-purchase WhatsApp or email sequence specifically designed to trigger order two. Test the timing, the offer, and the product recommendation. This single change has cut payback periods in half for several brands we've worked with.

The Takeaway

Report your CAC if you must. But manage your Payback Period. When someone asks you if your marketing is working, the honest answer lives in that number — not in your reported ROAS, and not in your blended CAC. How long does it take for a new customer to pay you back what you spent to get them? If you don't know that number, you are flying blind.
